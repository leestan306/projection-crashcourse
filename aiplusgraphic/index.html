Part 1 · Prompt Engineering

What is Prompt Engineering?

Prompt engineering is the skill of writing instructions to AI that produce the exact output you need — instead of generic, vague, or wrong results.

Think of AI as a very smart assistant who does exactly what you say — but takes you literally. Better instructions = better results.

Analogy: A bad creative brief gives you a bad design. A sharp creative brief gives you a sharp design. Prompting works the same way.
Vague prompt
"Make a poster" → generic, forgettable output
Engineered prompt
Role + context + constraints + format → precise, useful output
5

Part 1 · Prompt Engineering

The 5 Elements of a strong prompt

  • R
    Role — Tell AI who to be. "Act as a senior graphic designer." "Act as a brand strategist."
  • C
    Context — Give the background. What is this for? Who is the audience? What is the goal?
  • T
    Task — State clearly what you want. "Give me 5 poster concepts." "Write 3 headline variations."
  • F
    Format — Tell it how to structure the answer. Numbered list, bullet points, table, paragraph.
  • C
    Constraints — What to avoid or limit. "Avoid generic ideas." "No more than 6 words per headline."
Remember: You don't need all 5 every time. But the more you include, the sharper the output.

✅ With role

Context & specificity

AI cannot read your mind. The more context you give, the more useful the output.

❌ Too vague
"Give me color palette ideas."
✅ With context
"Give me 3 color palette options for a luxury real estate brand targeting high-income buyers in Nairobi. Include hex codes. The tone should feel premium, modern, and trustworthy."
Context checklist: Who is it for? What is the goal? What tone? What industry? What platform (print, Instagram, billboard)?

C

Part 1 · Prompt Engineering

Constraints keep AI focused

Without constraints, AI gives you safe, generic answers. Constraints force creativity and relevance.

❌ No constraints
"Suggest font pairings for my poster."
✅ With constraints
"Suggest 3 font pairings for a bold, dark-themed music event poster. Avoid overused fonts like Bebas Neue or Montserrat. Each pairing must include a headline font and a body font. Explain why they work together in one sentence."

Useful constraint phrases:

  • →
    "Avoid generic ideas"
  • →
    "No more than [X] words"
  • →
    "Do not suggest [X]"
  • →
    "Each option must be distinct from the others"
  • →
    "Be brutally honest" / "Be concise"

Tell AI exactly how to structure its response — it changes how usable the output is.

Part 1 · Prompt Engineering

Chain-of-thought prompting

Ask AI to reason through a problem step by step — don't just ask for the answer. This gets far better results for complex design decisions.

❌ Direct question
"What layout should I use for this poster?"
✅ Chain of thought
"I'm designing a concert poster. The headline is 'ONE NIGHT ONLY', the date is 14 June, and the artist name is KIMANI. Think through the visual hierarchy step by step: what should be largest, what should be secondary, and where should the supporting details go? Then give me a final layout recommendation."
Add these phrases: "Think step by step." / "Reason through this before answering." / "Explain your logic, then give the answer."

Part 1 · Prompt Engineering

Iteration — refining the output

One prompt rarely gives you the perfect answer. Think of it as a conversation — each response is a draft you refine.

1
First prompt
Get a starting point. Don't expect perfection.
2
Critique it
"Option 3 is too generic. Make it more symbolic and bold."
3
Constrain it
"Now rewrite options 1 and 3, but keep them under 6 words each."
4
Combine
"Take the tension from option 1 and the typography direction from option 4 and merge them."
Key phrase: "Keep what works, change what doesn't — and explain what you changed."

Part 2 · The Problem

Why AI posters look generic

Fully AI-generated posters look AI-ish for specific, fixable reasons:

  • ✕
    Too many effects competing at once
  • ✕
    Over-detailed, busy backgrounds
  • ✕
    Poor typography — fonts that don't match the tone
  • ✕
    No visual hierarchy — everything shouting equally
  • ✕
    No restraint — nothing removed
  • ✕
    Stock-looking focal images with no concept behind them
The shift: You are not using AI to design. You are using AI to think faster. The creative judgment is still yours.

Phase 1 · Strategy

Phase 1 — Strategy
before opening Photoshop

AI should define clarity before you touch any design tool. This prevents random designing.

Strategy Builder Prompt
I am designing a poster for: [event / business / campaign] Target audience: [age, interests, context] Goal: [sell tickets / raise awareness / launch product] Tone: [bold / spiritual / luxury / street / corporate] Give me: 1. A clear visual hierarchy (what should be biggest to smallest) 2. The emotional direction this poster should create 3. 3 distinct creative angles to explore 4. A suggested focal point idea 5. Color direction with 3–4 hex codes

Phase 2 · Focal Concept

Phase 2 — The big
focal idea

This is where most AI posters fail — a generic "brain graphic" or "globe icon" instead of something symbolic and memorable.

Focal Concept Generator
Act as a creative director at a top design agency. Give me 5 strong visual focal point ideas for a poster about: [topic] Rules: - Avoid generic, stock-photo-style ideas - Make each idea symbolic, bold, or metaphorical - Each should be visually striking and uncluttered - Think like a designer, not a content writer
Example: Instead of "AI brain image" → a glowing fingerprint made of code · a cracked wall revealing circuitry · a human shadow shaped like a robot

Phase 3 · Inspiration

Phase 3 — Pinterest
keyword mining

You don't search "church poster." You search mood-based keywords.

Make them aesthetic and mood-based.

Phase 6a · Layout

Phase 6 — Layout
blueprint

Before designing, have AI map out the spatial layout using the 8 composition rules. This prevents messy, unstructured work.

Layout Blueprint Prompt
Based on these poster elements: Headline: "[your headline]" Subtext: "[event details / tagline]" Call to action: "[website / date / ticket link]" Focal image: [describe the main visual] Describe a clean layout structure using: - Visual hierarchy (what is biggest to smallest) - Grid alignment (centred / rule of thirds / off-grid) - White space placement - Focal point position - Typography zones Be specific about placement. No vague advice.

Phase 6b · Evaluation

Final step —
AI poster critique

Upload your poster to AI and ask for a brutal, structured critique. This is how you improve fast.

Final Critique Prompt
Act as a senior graphic designer and harsh design critic. Critique this poster based on: 1. Visual hierarchy — is the eye guided correctly? 2. Focal point — is there one clear hero element? 3. Balance — does the layout feel stable? 4. Typography — do the fonts match the tone? 5. White space — is it used or wasted? 6. Alignment — is everything intentionally placed? 7. Contrast — is text readable against the background? 8. Professionalism — does it look competent? Be brutally honest. Tell me what feels amateur. Tell me what to remove. Tell me the one most important change to make.
PS

Design Execution

Inside Photoshop —
the right order

Always build your poster in this order. Each step sets up the next.

  • 1
    Background first — set the mood with colour and tone before anything else
  • 2
    Add texture — overlay grain, paper, or concrete with Soft Light or Overlay blend mode
  • 3
    Place the focal element — your hero image, shape, or illustration
  • 4
    Add the headline (BIG) — this should command the most visual weight
  • 5
    Add supporting text — smaller, quieter, secondary
  • 6
    Adjust contrast — push darks darker and lights lighter until it pops
  • 7
    Add grain overlay — this unifies everything and kills the "digital" feel
  • 8
    Step back and remove ONE thing — if you don't remove something, it's probably cluttered
